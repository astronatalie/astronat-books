Arithmetical Questions, on a New Plan: Designed as a Supplement to the Author's Engraved Introduction to Arithmetic; and Intended to Answer the Double Purpose of Arithmetical Instruction and Miscellaneous Information to which are Subjoined, Observations on Weights and Measures, with a Complete Collection of Arithmetical Tables, and Explanatory Remarks. For the Use of Young Ladies.

William Butler, 1795 (Second, Elarged Edition)

Arithmetical Questions (Second, Enlarged Edition, 1795) is the oldest book in my collection, and one of my absolute favourites - although maths isn't my main area of collection, this book is just such an amazing relic of the past.
To begin with the book itself, before I even start on the content, the covers are beautifully marbled and still in quite great condition despite being 229 years old. The spine leather is a bit flaky, and the paper title is partially detached, but the wonderful people at Douglas Stewart Fine Books made sure to preserve it in a plastic cover, which I will definitely adopt for some of my other books. My favourite part about the aesthetics of this book, however, is the paper - the entire book is written on a pale eggshell blue paper, something I had never seen nor heard of before. All pages are clean, with the exception of some doodles written in fountain pen, including an entire question with a large X through it - I guess even 18th-century students got frustrated over their maths homework! Lastly, it wasn't until the turn of the 19th-century that the long s was phased out of typography, and thus trying to skim read the text gives one a headache: "By the folar fyftem is meant the order and difpofition of the feveral heavenly bodies [...] Thefe celeftial fpheres confift of planets and comets." Of course, I can't replicate the true text on my modern laptop, but the long s is near indistinguishable in print to the regular f. Imagine a word like selflessness - felfleffneff!
Okay, now on to the actual content - this book contains a range of problems on a range of topics, from the Battle of Hastings, to Halley's Comet, to the price of stockings (something all women MUST know!). I was very surprised as to the attitude the author took towards the education of women; despite the views of the time, William Butler ("teacher of writing, accounts, and geography, in ladies schools, and in private families") states the education of women as being "eminently conjucive to the welfare of a state". He does, however, go on to justify that claim by quoting "[women] are only competent to form men"... lovely. Although Butler was heavily in support for the education of women, he did so under the intent to provide young men with higher quality early education. But hey, it was a start!

The Young Gentleman's Book (Containing a Series of Choice Readings in Popular Science and Natural History)

1834 (Second Edition)

The Young Gentleman's Book Containing a Series of Choice Lectures in Popular History and Natural History, Together with Retrospective Essays, Conversations, Literary Reminiscences, Etc. (Second Edition, 1834) is one of my oldest books, and definitely the catalyst for my obsession with 19th-century volumes - it doesn't quite fit with what I usually collect, but that beautiful marbling convinced me to buy it.
Covering a vast range of topics (and I mean that in the most literal sense), this guidebook introduces topics in geology, astronomy, biology, chemistry and physics, aimed at "young gentlemen".
Possibly it's most unique feature is a chapter covering over 150 "Conversational Topics", covering everything from the Aurora Borealis to stilton cheese (which can conveniently be found on the same page). [include quote about women and books]
